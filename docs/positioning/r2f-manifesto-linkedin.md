For fifteen years, the two sharpest minds in decision science disagreed about the single most important question in strategy: can you trust your gut?

Daniel Kahneman spent his career proving that you can't. Cognitive biases — anchoring, overconfidence, sunk-cost, groupthink — systematically distort the reasoning of even the most experienced executives. His answer was rigor. Debias the judgment. Measure the noise. Protect the decision from the decision-maker.

Gary Klein spent his career proving that you can. Twenty years of pattern-matching in firefighter command rooms, emergency rooms, and military operations rooms taught him that expert intuition isn't a flaw — it's the sharpest tool an experienced operator has. Trust the recognition. Amplify the pattern. The veteran CSO sees things the junior analyst can't.

In 2009 they published a paper together titled "Conditions for Intuitive Expertise: a failure to disagree." That title was a joke at their own expense. They had sat in a room for months and couldn't reconcile the two traditions — even though the field needed the reconciliation badly.

We built it.

At Decision Intel, every strategic memo passes through what we call the Recognition-Rigor Framework — R²F for short. The rigor side runs Kahneman's tradition: a bias detective trained on the 22-bias DI-B taxonomy, a noise judge that runs three decorrelated samples (analyst-skeptical / regulator-hostile / contrarian-strategist frames across two model architectures) on the same memo, a statistical jury that checks whether the evidence actually supports the argument's confidence level. The recognition side runs Klein's tradition: an RPD recognition engine that pattern-matches against 143 audited historical corporate decisions, a forgotten-questions node that surfaces what a veteran operator would have asked, a pre-mortem step that imagines the outcome backwards. A meta-judge arbitrates.

The output isn't one score. It's the reasoning reinforced from both sides.

A CSO reading their own memo through R²F doesn't hear "your thinking is flawed." They see the patterns they've earned over twenty years of reps getting amplified on one panel, and the blind spots that have been quietly accumulating getting flagged on the other. Their judgment stays in the centre. It's just reinforced now — from both traditions, never replaced.

No other vendor in the decision-quality space runs both halves. Cloverpop logs decisions. Palantir analyzes data. IBM Watson does generic AI. McKinsey Decision Analytics consults quarterly. Every one of them picks a side — and in picking, they leave half the decision underserved.

This matters because the real competitor in corporate strategy isn't any of those vendors. It's decision archaeology: the four-tool graveyard every strategy team uses to "remember how we decided." The Google Doc where the draft lived. The Slack thread where the feedback arrived. The Confluence writeup that captured the what but never the why. The board deck that presented the decision without the thinking. Six months later when an outcome lands, the rationale is buried across four hundred messages and twelve revisions, and the honest answer to "why did we enter Brazil in 2024?" becomes "we'd have to ask the person who wrote it — and she left last quarter."

R²F replaces that archaeology with a system of record. One place where the memo lives, the reasoning is audited, and the decision joins a living knowledge graph that compounds quarter after quarter.
