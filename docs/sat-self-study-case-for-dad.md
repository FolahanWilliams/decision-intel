# Why I'm self-studying for the SAT — and not getting a tutor

_A short, concrete case + plan. Written to talk through with Dad._

## The goal

**1280 → 1550.** That's the band Stanford and other test-requiring private universities expect. Worth knowing up front: **UC Berkeley is test-blind** — it won't even look at an SAT score — so this effort is specifically for the Stanford-tier schools that _do_ require it. (I'll re-check Stanford's exact policy closer to my application, but the test-requiring privates are the target either way.)

This isn't a tune-up. At 1550 you can only miss a handful of questions on the entire test, so the work is precise, not broad.

## Why a lesson teacher is the wrong tool for _this_ gap

**1. The gap isn't missing content — it's my specific recurring mistakes.** Going from 1280 to 1550 isn't about learning new material; it's about finding the exact patterns I get wrong and drilling them out. A teacher lectures to the average student at a fixed pace and gives feedback days later. That's the wrong _shape_ of help for a precision problem.

**2. No teacher can beat the official materials — and they're free.** The digital SAT is _adaptive_ (the questions change based on how you're doing), and the only true-to-test practice is the College Board's own **Bluebook** app and **Khan Academy's official** course — both built by the people who write the exam, both free. A paid tutor can't recreate the real adaptive test; at best they hand over worksheets that are slightly off from the real thing.

**3. Independent research finds paid coaching gains are modest.** Studies of commercial test prep have repeatedly found the _average_ score improvement is far smaller than the marketing promises. The students who make big jumps are the ones doing focused, self-directed practice with ruthless error review — which is exactly what I've set up.

## This isn't winging it — it's a more rigorous system than tutoring

I've built a study system that does what a weekly teacher structurally can't:

- It **logs every question I miss** and tags _why_ I missed it (didn't know it / careless / misread / fell for a trap / ran out of time).
- It **schedules each miss for spaced review** on a forgetting-curve algorithm, so I re-encounter what I got wrong right before I'd forget it.
- It **tracks my calibration** — where I'm _confident and wrong_, which is where points quietly disappear — and targets those first.

That's personalised to _my_ error patterns in a way an hour-a-week tutor never could be. It's grounded in the actual learning science that moves scores: retrieval practice (testing yourself beats re-reading), spacing, interleaving, and calibration.

## The plan — 30 focused minutes a day

- **Now:** one official Bluebook full-length, timed, to get my real Reading↔Math split and weakest skills.
- **Summer:** daily targeted drilling on my weakest skills + spaced review of past misses. Lock **Math toward 790–800 first** (it's finite, pattern-based, and my strongest area), then grind Reading & Writing.
- **September:** sit the real SAT as a live-conditions benchmark. (Stanford superscores — takes your best section across dates — so this one counts toward the final.)
- **November:** the real push. **This is the score that matters.**

## On accountability — this is _more_ accountable than a tutor, not less

Every study session, every missed question, and every practice-test score is logged and tracked, with a daily streak. There's no hiding whether I'm doing the work — the data shows it, and you can see it any time.

## The safety valve — this makes the decision low-risk

We don't have to bet everything on self-study. **Judge it by the September benchmark.** If I'm on a clear upward path toward 1550, the system is working. If I've stalled, that's the trigger to bring in _targeted_ help — a specialist for the one section that's stuck, not a generic weekly tutor.

**Measure first, then decide.** That costs nothing and risks nothing — and it puts the decision on real data instead of a guess we'd make today.
